Frequently Asked Questions

Preventing plant diseases involves several practices: choose disease-resistant varieties, ensure proper spacing for air circulation, water at the base of plants rather than overhead, practice crop rotation, remove and dispose of infected plant material, and maintain good garden hygiene. Regular monitoring helps catch problems early.

Bacterial diseases often appear as water-soaked lesions that may ooze or have a yellow halo. They commonly occur during warm, wet weather. Fungal diseases typically show powdery or fuzzy growth, distinct spot patterns, or visible spore structures. Treatment methods differ, with bacterial diseases requiring copper-based products and fungal diseases responding to specific fungicides.

Organic treatments can be effective, especially when applied preventatively or at the first sign of disease. Options include neem oil, copper and sulfur-based products, potassium bicarbonate, and biological controls. Their effectiveness depends on timing, coverage, disease pressure, and consistent application. Integrating cultural practices with organic treatments usually yields the best results.

Plant pathogens generally don't infect humans. The organisms that cause plant diseases have evolved to specifically infect plant cells, which are structurally different from human cells. However, some fungi that grow on plants can produce toxins (mycotoxins) that are harmful if consumed, and some people may develop allergic reactions to plant molds and spores.
